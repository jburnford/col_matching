Stage 1 — The local model

Running entirely on this machine, a local Qwen 3.6 structurer read each printed biography and emitted a structured record: birth year, a timeline of career events (position, place, start/end year, acting flag), honours, and a terminal entry. The source text is hard: 1860s–1960s print, scanned and OCR‑converted, packed with abbreviations — E.A.P. for East Africa Protectorate, S. Sttlmts. for Straits Settlements, date ranges compressed to 1900-02 or even 1896-7-8.

The flag-don't-drop discipline

A separate deterministic validator then checked every extracted value against the printed source, under a strict rule: a fact the source does not literally support is never silently kept. Each unconfirmed value is flagged rather than deleted, so nothing is lost and a second pass can adjudicate it. The validator requires:

  • every event year (year_start/year_end) to appear verbatim in the source;
  • every non-inherited place to appear token-wise in the source;
  • birth year, honour award-years and the terminal year to appear in the source.

This flagged 2,639 facts across 1,286 persons — the work-list for Stage 2. The flags split as:

Many flags are false alarms caused by OCR and abbreviation, not genuine extraction errors: the validator can't see that E.A.P. & T. admin. in the source supports the value “E.A.P. & T. administration,” or that the printed range 1896-7-8 really does contain the year 1898. Separating those from real errors is exactly what Stage 2 does.

Stage 2 — The careful review

Every flagged fact was adjudicated keep / correct / drop, and — critically — each keep or correct had to cite an exact substring of that person's source text as evidence. A final Python merge re-checks every quote and only restores a value whose evidence is genuinely a substring of the source. The model proposes; Python keeps the last word, preserving a zero‑false‑positive guarantee.

Why things were dropped (494)

Drops are genuine errors the source does not support. The dominant patterns:

  • Inferred year-ends — the structurer guessed a role ended the year before the next began; that year is nowhere in the print.
  • OCR-truncated years — fragments like “B. 18” or a bare “1” that aren't real four-digit years.
  • Abbreviation mis-reads — col. (colonel) or prot. (protector) wrongly turned into a place like “colonial” or “protectorate.”
  • Place inferences — e.g. “Canada” assigned to a federal office where only “privy council” appears in the source.

The merge folded 2,111 quote-verified values back into the corpus → llm_struct_corpus.reviewed.jsonl (36,167 records, line-for-line matching the validated corpus). The 31 keep/correct verdicts not restored were whole-event flags whose year_start is a non-integer string (e.g. “October, 1892”) — the gate declines to auto-restore those, the conservative behaviour. A fresh grounding work-list of 12,591 distinct ungrounded places was regenerated on the reviewed output.

Zero false positives preserved. Every one of the 2,111 restorations is backed by an exact substring of the person's printed biography, re-checked at merge time. A spot-check of 10 random keep/correct verdicts confirmed all 10 quotes are genuinely present in source.

A note on how Stage 2 actually ran

The original plan fanned out 17 independent Sonnet review agents in parallel. After a usage-limit incident and a wedged background-agent runner, the approach was revised mid-flight: a free deterministic pre-filter was added to absorb the mechanical cases, and the residue was reviewed in-session by the main model in small, durable, incrementally-written batches. Three agent batches (006/014/017) survive from the first attempt; the rest were done in the main loop. The pre-filter and the two-phase, resumable runbook are now committed to the repository.
